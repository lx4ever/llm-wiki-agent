### Chapter 2: The Councilor's Intuition

The next day found me in the local archives, the photograph of the boy and the councillor clutched in my hand. Among the musty pages and faded ink, I searched for any clue that might lead me to understand my time-traveling experiences.

Hours passed until a name caught my eye: Lillian Grayson, a councillor known in the late Victorian era for her work with London's underprivileged children. The more I read, the more intrigued I became. She was a woman ahead of her time, fiercely intelligent and deeply empathetic.

Determined to learn more, I ventured to the address listed in the archives, now a modern apartment complex.

As I stood there, memories of the previous day's encounter swirling in my mind, the world shifted again, I experienced the familiar, disorienting pull of time travel once more.

I found myself back in the Victorian era, outside a modest brick house. Hesitantly, I knocked. The door opened, and there stood Lillian Grayson, just as I had seen her in the photograph.

"Miss Grayson," I began, "I'm sorry to intrude, but I need to speak with you."

Her gaze was piercing, as if she could see right through me. "I've been expecting you," she said, her voice calm. "Please, come in."

Inside, the air was thick with the scent of herbs and ink. Books and papers cluttered every surface. Lillian motioned for me to sit.

"You saw the boy," she stated, more a fact than a question. "You know, don't you?"

I nodded, pulling out the photograph. "I don't understand how, but I travel through time. I saw you with him. You said, 'I know.' What did you mean?"

Lillian's eyes softened as she took the photograph, tracing the outline of the boy's face with her finger. "I have a gift, or a curse, depending on one's perspective. I can sense things about people, their emotions, their truths. That boy, he's caught in something dark, something beyond his control."

A chill ran down my spine. "What happened to him?"

She sighed, a weight of sadness in her eyes. "I don't have all the answers. But I suspect his fate is intertwined with someone else's, someone with a heart filled with darkness."

The room grew colder, and I felt the edges of my vision start to blur. Time was pulling me back.

"Wait!" I exclaimed. "Who is this person with a dark heart?"

But Lillian Grayson was already fading, her final words echoing in the shifting room, "Find the boy, and you find the killer."

I was back in the present, the photograph still in my hand. Lillian's words haunted me. The boy, the killer, the mystery - it was all connected. And I knew then that I couldn't let it go. I had to find out the truth, no matter where it led me.

The subsequent day found me entrenched within the dusty alcoves of the local archives, tightly clutching the enigmatic photograph of the boy and the councillor. Amidst the scent of ancient parchment and the faded ink of forgotten stories, I sought tirelessly for any hint or shred of insight that might help me unravel the perplexing mystery of my perplexing time-traveling experiences.

The hands of the clock moved in a slow, relentless march as hours turned into a blur of relentless research. Suddenly, a name leapt out from the sea of text and caught my attention: Lillian Grayson, a prominent councillor renowned in the late Victorian era for her tireless and selfless work with London's destitute and underprivileged children. As I delved deeper into the annals of her story, my intrigue grew. She was a woman far ahead of her time, a beacon of intelligence and a wellspring of empathy.

Feeling a renewed determination to uncover more about this fascinating character, I took a bold step. I journeyed to the address listed in the old archives, which, in the present day, was transformed into a sleek, modern apartment complex.

Standing there, with the vivid memories of my previous day's encounter dancing in my mind, I felt the world around me shift once again. The familiar, unsettling sensation of time travel washed over me, pulling me back into the heart of the Victorian era.

I found myself standing outside a modest brick house, the incongruity of my modern attire against the archaic backdrop not lost on me. With a sense of trepidation, I knocked on the heavy, wooden door. It creaked open to reveal Lillian Grayson, her countenance exactly as I had seen in the photograph.

"Miss Grayson," I began, my voice laced with an apology for my intrusion, "I'm sorry to impose, but it's imperative that I speak with you."

Her gaze was intense and penetrating, as though she held the power to see my very soul. "I've been expecting you," she responded, her voice steady and calm. "Please, come in."

Stepping inside, I was immediately enveloped by the pungent aroma of herbs mingled with the distinctive scent of ink. Books and papers were strewn across every conceivable surface, a testimony to her intellectual pursuits. Lillian gestured towards a chair, inviting me to sit.

"You saw the boy," she stated, her voice carrying an air of certainty that suggested she was making a statement rather than asking a question. "You are aware, aren't you?"

I nodded, pulling out the now well-worn photograph. "I'm at a loss to explain how, but I seem to be able to travel through time. I saw you with him. You uttered, 'I know.' What did you mean by that?"

Lillian's austere expression softened as she took the photograph from me, her fingers gently tracing the outline of the boy's face. "I possess a unique ability, or perhaps a burden, depending on one's viewpoint. I can sense things about individuals, their deepest emotions, their hidden truths. That boy, he's ensnared in a dark, ominous situation, something far beyond his control."

A chill coursed through my body. "What happened to him?" I managed to ask.

She expelled a melancholic sigh, her eyes heavy with sorrow. "I don't possess all the answers you seek. But it's my belief that his destiny is inextricably linked to another's, someone whose heart is steeped in darkness."

Suddenly, the room felt colder, and the edges of my vision began to blur. I could feel time calling me back to my present.

"Wait!" I cried out. "Who is this person with a dark heart?"

But my questions fell on deaf ears as Lillian Grayson was already fading into the past, her final words echoing hauntingly in the shifting space, "Find the boy, and you find the killer."

Within moments, I found myself back in the present day, the photograph still clutched in my hand. Lillian's words echoed in my mind incessantly. The boy, the killer, the mystery - everything was interconnected. And, in that moment, I made a solemn vow to myself: I could not and would not let it go. I had to uncover the truth, no matter where this daunting journey led me.
